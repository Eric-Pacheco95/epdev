## Articles (Direct -- No Sub-Domain Threshold Met)

### The Iran Trap (2026-03-30)
- Three distinct power structures (lobbying, financial, geopolitical) converge to push the US toward conflict with Iran regardless of strategic merit -- structural incentives override rational cost-benefit analysis.
- Historical overextension patterns (Athens-Sicily, Soviet Afghanistan) suggest the US risks repeating the same trap: asymmetric warfare dramatically raises occupier costs while lowering attacker costs.
- Asymmetric warfare shifts cost-benefit calculations such that a militarily weaker state can impose unsustainable costs on a stronger occupying power through attrition, proxy networks, and denial strategies.

### AI Datacenter Delays as Geopolitical Signal (2026-04-02)
- 30-50% of planned datacenter builds delayed or canceled signals that AI infrastructure investment may be outpacing near-term demand -- a potential bubble correction with downstream implications for US tech supremacy narratives.
- Capital flight from hyperscale AI infrastructure could shift competitive dynamics in the US-China AI race if the slowdown is asymmetric across actors.
- Energy constraints, permitting bottlenecks, and demand uncertainty are structural (not purely cyclical) drag factors on datacenter deployment timelines.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

1. Structural forces over rational actors: Both articles show institutional and structural forces driving outcomes independent of strategic merit -- lobbying coalitions push toward conflict; hype cycles push toward overbuilding. Individual rational actors cannot reverse structural momentum alone.
2. Overextension risk: Military overextension (Iran) and capital overextension (AI datacenters) follow similar dynamics -- commitments made under optimistic assumptions that become politically and financially costly to exit.
3. Asymmetric cost structures: Asymmetric warfare and asymmetric AI investment returns both illustrate how weaker or smaller players can impose disproportionate costs on dominant actors.
4. Narrative vs. reality lag: Media and political narratives (Iran threat framing, AI dominance) lag structural realities -- by the time consensus forms, the strategic window has often already closed or shifted.
