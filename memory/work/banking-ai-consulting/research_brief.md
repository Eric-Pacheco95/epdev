## Executive Summary

Canadian banks are spending aggressively on AI — RBC alone spends C$5B/year on technology and expects $700M-$1B in AI-generated value by 2027. TD targets $1B in combined revenue growth and cost savings from AI. Yet the consulting landscape serving these banks is dominated by Big 4 firms (Accenture, Deloitte, KPMG, PwC) and mid-size IT consultancies (CGI, Spiria). There is a **clear gap at the practitioner level**: no prominent solo consultants or boutique firms in Canada specialize in hands-on AI automation for banking operations (vs. strategy decks). The regulatory environment (OSFI Guideline E-23, open banking framework) is creating *new* compliance demand that banks must address by May 2027. The side-hustle feasibility depends entirely on Eric's specific employer's outside employment policy — Big 5 banks universally require disclosure and approval of outside business activities, and consulting for competing FIs while employed would almost certainly be blocked. However, adjacent markets (fintechs, credit unions, non-competing FIs, insurance) and non-consulting revenue paths (courses, templates, content) are viable.

## Market & Opportunity

### Bank AI Spending (demand signal is real)

- **RBC**: C$5B+/year on technology; AI research lab (Borealis) since 2016; Aiden trading platform; "North for Banking" partnership with Cohere; ranked #3 globally, #1 in Canada for AI maturity (Evident index)
- **TD**: ~C$4B tech spend (est. 2026); acquired Layer 6 AI research firm (2018); TD AI Prism predictive sales tool; targeting $1B total AI value (cost savings + revenue growth); deploying agentic AI in back-end operations to reduce expenses by C$2-2.5B
- **BMO, CIBC, Scotiabank**: All investing heavily; CIBC's generative AI platform piloted across all lines of business since July 2024; Scotiabank dropped in Evident rankings (29th) signaling potential catch-up spending ahead
- **Industry-wide**: Canada ranked highest-scoring region globally for AI maturity across 50 international banks. KPMG reports "rapid acceleration of investor interest in AI-focused fintechs" in Canada. Fintech CVC investment up to US$313M across 25 deals in 2025

### The Unstructured Gap (where the real pain is)

Banking automation has **stalled on unstructured data**. Legacy RPA/bots handle structured Column A → Column B flows well. But compliance data arrives as:
- Scanned passports and driver's licenses (KYC)
- PDF deeds and trust documents (wealth management)
- Free-text emails about suspicious transactions (fraud)
- PDF regulatory updates from government bodies

This creates **"Shadow Compliance"** — armies of highly paid analysts manually reviewing documents because they can't trust their software. AI agents that can reason over unstructured documents are the 2026 breakthrough.

### Specific Pain Points (highest underserved value)

1. **KYC Refresh** — banks must periodically refresh customer data; manual process is expensive and error-prone; AI can parse unstructured identity documents
2. **AML Transaction Monitoring** — legacy rules-based systems generate massive false positives; AI reduces noise while catching real threats
3. **Regulatory Change Management** — OSFI alone produces dozens of guideline updates annually; banks manually track and implement changes
4. **Fraud Detection** — cross-channel, AI-driven fraud is outpacing rule-based defenses; banks need adaptive AI monitoring
5. **Reporting Automation** — internal compliance reporting, risk reporting, management information; high-volume, document-heavy, currently manual

## Regulatory Environment (creates demand, not just constraints)

### OSFI Guideline E-23 — Model Risk Management (effective May 1, 2027)

- Applies to ALL models at ALL federally regulated financial institutions (banks, insurance, trust companies)
- Requires enterprise-wide model risk management frameworks covering AI/ML models through full lifecycle
- Key challenge: operationalizing requirements for **third-party "black box" models** where transparency is limited
- Banks need multi-disciplinary teams (including legal and ethics) to assess model risks
- **Consulting opportunity**: Most banks lack internal expertise to inventory, validate, and govern their growing AI model portfolios. This is a **compliance deadline** — not optional

### Consumer-Driven Banking Act (Open Banking — launching 2026)

- Read Access phase expected early 2026; Write Access phase tied to Real-Time Rail (RTR) deployment in 2027
- Banks must build compliant API infrastructure, consent management, third-party accreditation
- ~9 million Canadians currently share banking credentials via screen scraping (the unsafe status quo)
- Competition Bureau estimates data portability could save Canadians C$1.1-3.8B annually
- **Consulting opportunity**: Banks need API architecture, consent management, security audits — fintech partners need accreditation support

### AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act)

- Federal AI legislation has **stalled** — OSFI E-23 is filling the regulatory gap for financial services specifically
- Quebec AMF has its own AI risk management framework for FIs in that province

- The practitioner-who-actually-builds gap is real, especially at the credit union / mid-tier FI / fintech level where Big 4 pricing is prohibitive

## Side-Hustle Feasibility

### Employment Conflict Analysis (CRITICAL)

**All Big 5 Canadian banks require:**
- Disclosure of outside employment/business activities
- Prior approval from a designated authority
- Assessment of real, apparent, or potential conflicts of interest

**What would likely be BLOCKED:**
- Consulting directly for another Big 5 bank or direct competitor
- Any work that uses proprietary information, systems, or processes from your employer
- Work that could appear to influence your job duties or create divided loyalty

**What is MORE LIKELY APPROVED (with disclosure):**
- Teaching/training courses on general AI automation (not bank-specific)
- Consulting for non-competing industries (fintechs that aren't direct competitors, insurance, credit unions in other provinces)
- Creating and selling educational content, templates, frameworks
- Open-source contributions

**Key rule**: You MUST disclose and get approval BEFORE starting. The designated authority decides — not you. Proceeding without approval is a fireable offense at every Big 5 bank.

## Open Banking as Adjacent Opportunity

Canada's Consumer-Driven Banking framework launching in 2026 creates a **time-bounded opportunity**:
- Banks must build compliant API infrastructure
- Fintechs must achieve accreditation as third-party providers
- Both sides need people who understand banking operations AND technical implementation
- Write Access (payment initiation, account funding) arrives with RTR in 2027 — second wave of demand
- Competition Bureau is pushing urgency: concentrated market, high barriers, real switching friction

This is relevant because: someone who understands both the banking side (operations, compliance, customer data) AND the technical side (APIs, AI, automation) is exactly what both banks and fintechs need during this transition.

## Risks & Hard Parts

1. **Employment agreement is the #1 blocker** — must disclose, may be restricted from the highest-value work (consulting for competing FIs). Get this answered first before investing time
2. **Big 4 credibility moat** — enterprise buyers default to known brands for risk management; solo consultants need exceptional proof-of-work to compete for bank contracts
3. **Compliance complexity** — OSFI E-23, privacy laws (PIPEDA, Quebec Law 25), AML/KYC regulations create liability risk for consultants giving bad advice
4. **Sales cycle** — even mid-tier FIs have procurement processes; 3-6 month sales cycles are common; cash flow is lumpy
5. **Non-compete risk** — even after leaving the bank, non-compete or non-solicitation clauses may restrict client access for 6-24 months
6. **Course/template market saturation** — generic "AI for business" courses are everywhere; differentiation requires genuine banking domain specificity

## Entry Point Recommendation

### Lowest-Risk, Highest-Signal First Move

**Option A: Internal path (zero conflict risk)**
Pitch an AI automation project at your own bank. You have a working system (Jarvis), you understand the pain points, and success here is the most powerful proof-of-work possible. This is a career accelerator even if you never consult externally.

**Option B: Education-first (low conflict risk)**
Build a course or content series: "AI Automation for Financial Services Professionals." General education, not bank-specific. Builds audience and credibility. Revenue is modest but recurring. Positions you as a domain expert.

**Option C: Credit union / small FI consulting (medium conflict risk, requires approval)**
Credit unions and smaller FIs can't afford Big 4 but desperately need AI/automation help, especially with OSFI E-23 compliance (May 2027 deadline). Non-competing, likely approvable. $150-200/hr starting.

**Recommended sequence**: A first (internal credibility), B in parallel (audience building), C when ready to go external (or after leaving the bank).
