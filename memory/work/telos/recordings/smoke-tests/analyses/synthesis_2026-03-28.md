This synthesis is based on the analysis of your studio performance of "I'm Moving On" and your live performance of "Lilypad."

## Player Profile
*   **Overall Playing Level:** **Advanced-Intermediate.** You possess the technical facility to execute clean 16th-note syncopation and the musicality to construct memorable, vocal-like hooks. However, your "live" execution shows significant degradation in rhythmic discipline and pitch control, preventing a "professional" rating.
*   **Primary Style Identity:** **Indie-Fusion Hybrid.** You successfully blend the interlocking, rhythmic "grid" of The Strokes/The 1975 with the blues-informed melodicism of John Mayer and the fluid, modal approach of Jerry Garcia.
*   **Signature Strengths:** Your strongest asset is **vocal-centric phrasing.** You don't just "shred" scales; you compose melodies within your solos that mirror a singer’s cadence. Your articulation is consistently clean, even when using edge-of-breakup gain.

## Consistent Patterns
*   **Recurring Strengths:** Across both recordings, your **melodic hook construction** stands out. You have a knack for finding the "earworm" within a scale (A Mixolydian in "Lilypad," D Major in "I'm Moving On").
*   **Recurring Weaknesses:** 
    *   **Pitch Pathology:** You have a chronic issue with **bend intonation.** Full-step bends are consistently 10–15 cents flat. This is not a "blues curl" stylistic choice; it is an under-shooting of the target note that creates harmonic friction.
    *   **Nervous Vibrato:** Your vibrato is narrow and fast, bordering on a "tremble." It lacks the rhythmic width found in your influences (Mayer/Garcia) and makes your sustained notes feel anxious rather than authoritative.
*   **Tone & Timing:** You favor a **glassy, mid-forward Stratocaster tone** (Positions 2/4) that provides excellent clarity but offers no place to hide technical flaws. Your timing is consistently **"on top" of the beat.** While this drives the "Indie" side of your style, it makes the "Fusion/Soul" elements feel frantic and rushed.

## Development Priority Stack
1.  **Bend Intonation (The "Pitch Ceiling"):** Your flat bends are the single biggest factor holding you back from a professional sound.
    *   *Exercise:* **Unison Target Drills.** Play a target note (e.g., B on the 7th fret, E string). Then, bend the A on the 10th fret (B string) to match it. Use a strobe tuner. Do not stop until the "beating" between the notes disappears.
2.  **Rhythmic Pocket (The "Rush" Factor):** You push the beat during syncopated 16th runs, especially live.
    *   *Exercise:* **The "Behind the Click" Challenge.** Set a metronome to 60 BPM. Play 16th-note scales, but intentionally try to place the notes on the "back half" of the beat. Record it and listen back; you will likely find that "trying to play late" actually results in you finally being "on time."
3.  **Vibrato Development:** Your current vibrato sounds like a physical reflex rather than a musical choice.
    *   *Exercise:* **Metronomic Vibrato.** Set a click to 80 BPM. Strike a note and pulse your vibrato in perfect 8th notes, then triplets, then 16th notes. Focus on the *width* of the pitch excursion.
4.  **Chromatic Vocabulary:** You are currently "box-bound" to diatonic scales.
    *   *Exercise:* **Garcia Enclosures.** When targeting a chord tone (like the 3rd), play one fret above, one fret below, and then the target note (e.g., to hit C#, play D -> C -> C#). This will break the "scale-only" sound.
5.  **Dynamic Range:** Your performances lack a "narrative arc" because you stay at a constant volume.
    *   *Exercise:* **The 1-to-10 Drill.** Play a 4-bar loop. Bar 1 is volume 2 (barely audible), Bar 4 is volume 10 (aggressive). Use only your pick attack and guitar volume knob to achieve this.

## Studio vs. Live Comparison
*   **The "Adrenaline Tax":** In the studio ("I'm Moving On"), your 16th-note syncopation is crisp and professional. Live ("Lilypad"), that same syncopation becomes "trippy" and rushed. Your internal clock accelerates under pressure.
*   **Pitch Stability:** Your bend intonation is noticeably worse live. This suggests you are relying on visual cues or "muscle memory" rather than truly hearing the pitch through the stage monitors.

## Style Evolution Recommendations
To move from "Advanced-Intermediate" to "Expert," you must integrate your influences more deeply:
*   **From Garcia:** Study **chromatic leading tones.** You currently play "in the key," whereas Garcia played "to the chord." Start targeting the 3rd and 7th of every chord change using chromatic approaches.
*   **From Mayer:** Adopt his **percussive "slap-flick" technique.** Your rhythm playing is clean but "polite." Adding percussive rakes and thumb-slaps will give your Indie-Fusion style more "weight."
*   **From Wonder:** Analyze his **pentatonic substitutions.** Stevie often plays minor pentatonic a fifth away from the root to access extensions (e.g., E minor pentatonic over an A7 chord to get the 9th and 13th). This will modernize your "box" shapes.

**Bottom Line:** You have the "ears" for melody and the "fingers" for speed, but you lack the "discipline" of pitch and pocket. Fix the flat bends and the rushing, and your playing will transform.
