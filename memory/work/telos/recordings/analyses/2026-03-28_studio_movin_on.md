Here is the technical breakdown of your performance:

### Style & Influences
*   **Genre/Style**: Indie-Pop/Fusion Hybrid. It has the rhythmic urgency of early 2000s NYC indie rock (The Strokes, Phoenix) blended with the melodic lead approach of modern blues-fusion.
*   **Influences**:

    *   **The Strokes**: The interlocking, staccato rhythm parts in the background provide that driving, "on-top-of-the-beat" energy.

### Technique Assessment
*   **Phrasing**: Your phrases are melodic and "singable," which is a major strength. However, they often feel "breathless." You aren't leaving enough space for the listener to digest one idea before starting the next.
*   **Timing/Rhythm**: As noted in your profile, you are playing **very** "on top" of the beat. While this works for the genre, you are dangerously close to rushing during the 16th-note syncopated runs. The internal clock is pushing the tempo rather than sitting in the "pocket."
*   **Note Choice**: You rely heavily on the "Major Pentatonic Box 1" and "Mixolydian" shapes. While the notes are "correct," they are predictable. You are missing the "outside" flavors (chromatic passing tones) that would give this a true jazz/fusion edge.
*   **Dynamics**: The performance is "flat" at a volume of 9 or 10. There is very little difference in pick attack between the "verse" sections and the "solo" sections. 
*   **Articulation**: 
    *   **Bends**: Your full-step bends are consistently **10-15 cents flat**. You are reaching for the note but not quite "parking" it in the center of the pitch.
    *   **Vibrato**: It is a "nervous" vibrato㻖ast and narrow. It lacks the wide, confident "bloom" of a player like Paul Kossoff or even Mayer.

### Strengths

2.  **Clean Articulation**: Even when you are rushing, your note separation is excellent. There is no "mud" in your playing; every note is intended and heard.
3.  **Melodic Hook Construction**: You have a "pop" ear. Your lead lines aren't just scales; they are melodies that could be sung.

### Areas for Improvement (Specific & Actionable)

**1. Bend Intonation & "Target Practice"**
Your bends are the weakest link in your lead playing. You are relying on "feel" rather than "ear."
*   **Exercise**: **The Unison Bend Check.** Play a note on the B string, then bend the G string up to match it. Use a digital tuner (like a Peterson Strobe) and don't stop bending until the tuner turns green. Do this for 10 minutes a day. You need to "re-calibrate" your hand's muscle memory for what a true whole-step feels like.

**2. Rhythmic "Back-Slinging"**
You need to learn to play "behind the beat" to create a "soul" feel.
*   **Exercise**: Set a metronome to 60 BPM, but have it only click on **beat 4**. Play a simple 16th-note groove. If you are rushing, you will "hit" the click before it happens. Try to make your notes feel "heavy" and "lazy" without actually slowing down the tempo.

**3. Chromatic Enclosures (The Garcia/Jazz Element)**
To move beyond "box" playing, you need to target chord tones using chromaticism.
*   **Exercise**: Pick a target note (e.g., the 3rd of the chord). Practice "enclosing" it: play one fret above, one fret below, then the target note (e.g., to hit an E, play F -> D# -> E). Integrate this into your pentatonic scales to break up the "linear" feel.

### Overall Assessment
*   **Level**: **Advanced-Intermediate**
*   **One Sentence Summary**: To reach a professional level, you must prioritize **pitch-perfect bend intonation** and develop the ability to **play behind the beat** to add "weight" and "soul" to your technical proficiency.
